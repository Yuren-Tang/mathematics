# Quartic terminal nucleus and recursive peeling

## 1. Purpose

A quartic witness design on a set

$$
X,
\qquad
|X|=4k+1,
$$

has four near-resolutions

$$
\mathcal R_i
\qquad(i\in I=\{0,1,2,3\}),
$$

where `\mathcal R_i` partitions `X\setminus\{e_i\}` into `k` four-element blocks, the omitted points `e_i` are pairwise distinct, and all `4k` block vectors form a basis of the even-weight space

$$
E_X
=
\left\{x\in\mathbf F_2^X:\sum_{u\in X}x_u=0\right\}.
$$

The abstract ladder theorem shows that these incidence axioms do not bound `k`.  This chapter identifies the exact structure responsible for that unboundedness.

Every quartic design contains a canonical four-dimensional minus-type quadratic nucleus generated by the four whole-sheet block sums.  Its five singular points and ten anisotropic roots reproduce the intrinsic `E_5/K_5` geometry.  The route matching on the four sheet labels reduces the natural `S_4` coordinate symmetry of this nucleus to `D_8`.

The terminal points in each sheet then give a finite trichotomy.  If all four sheets are concentrated, the canonical nucleus peels off and leaves a strictly smaller quartic witness design.  Thus unbounded abstract cores arise by iterated nucleus extension; any failure of this recursion is exposed by root-valued split terminal blocks.

## 2. Quadratic structure on the even-weight space

For `x\in E_X`, define

$$
q_X(x)
=
\frac{|x|}{2}\pmod2.
$$

This is a quadratic form with polar form

$$
b_X(x,y)
=
q_X(x+y)+q_X(x)+q_X(y)
=
|x\cap y|\pmod2.
$$

Because `|X|` is odd, the dot product is nondegenerate on `E_X`: one has

$$
E_X^\perp=\langle1_X\rangle,
\qquad
1_X\notin E_X.
$$

Hence `(E_X,q_X)` is a nondegenerate quadratic space of dimension `4k`.

## 3. The canonical terminal nucleus

For each sheet put

$$
s_i
=
\sum_{B\in\mathcal R_i}1_B.
$$

Since `\mathcal R_i` partitions `X\setminus\{e_i\}`,

$$
\boxed{s_i=1_X+1_{e_i}.}
$$

### Theorem 3.1 — terminal nucleus

The vectors

$$
s_0,s_1,s_2,s_3
$$

are linearly independent and span a nondegenerate four-dimensional quadratic subspace

$$
S=\langle s_0,s_1,s_2,s_3\rangle
\le E_X
$$

of minus type.  More precisely,

$$
q_X(s_i)=0
$$

and, for `i\ne j`,

$$
\boxed{b_X(s_i,s_j)=1.}
$$

#### Proof

Each `s_i` has weight `4k`, so

$$
q_X(s_i)=2k=0\pmod2.
$$

For distinct `i,j`,

$$
\begin{aligned}
b_X(s_i,s_j)
&=(1_X+e_i)\cdot(1_X+e_j)\\
&=|X|+1+1+0\\
&=1\pmod2.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus the Gram matrix in the ordered basis `(s_0,s_1,s_2,s_3)` is

$$
K
=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&1&1&1\\
1&0&1&1\\
1&1&0&1\\
1&1&1&0
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Over `\mathbf F_2`, this matrix is invertible.  Indeed, if `Ka=0` and `A=\sum_i a_i`, then the `i`-th equation gives `a_i=A` for every `i`; summing gives `A=4A=0`, hence `a=0`.  Therefore `S` is four-dimensional and nondegenerate.

For a sum of exactly `m` distinct basis vectors `s_i`, the quadratic value is

$$
\binom m2\pmod2,
$$

because every `q_X(s_i)` is zero and every distinct pair has polar pairing one.  Hence the nonzero singular vectors in `S` are precisely the four `s_i` and their total sum

$$
s_\infty
=
s_0+s_1+s_2+s_3.
$$

There are exactly five nonzero singular vectors and ten anisotropic vectors.  Therefore `(S,q_X|_S)` is the four-dimensional minus-type quadratic space.  ∎

Since `S` is nondegenerate,

$$
\boxed{E_X=S\perp S^\perp.}
$$

This is an exact four-dimensional algebraic splitting.  It does not by itself imply that the residual summand carries smaller near-resolutions; that additional statement is proved in the concentrated case below.

## 4. The intrinsic `E_5/K_5` geometry

Label the five nonzero singular vectors by

$$
\{s_0,s_1,s_2,s_3,s_\infty\}.
$$

Their ten pairwise sums are exactly the ten anisotropic vectors of `S`.  Thus the complete anisotropic geometry is naturally the edge set of a `K_5` on these five singular points.

For `i\ne j`, six of the roots have the particularly simple form

$$
\boxed{s_i+s_j=e_i+e_j.}
$$

The remaining four are

$$
s_i+s_\infty
=
1_X+\sum_{j\ne i}e_j.
$$

Consequently every quartic witness design contains, inside its incidence coefficient space, a canonical copy of the same minus-type four-dimensional root geometry that defines five-support flows.

This is an incidence-space statement.  It does not yet identify the coefficient-space nucleus with a physical `E_5` flow on the original four-pole.

## 5. Route symmetry and the `D_8` stabiliser

A relabelling of the four sheets permutes the four vectors `s_i` and fixes `s_\infty`.  Since all off-diagonal pairings among the `s_i` are equal, every sheet permutation induces an isometry of `S`.  The resulting coordinate group is

$$
S_4
=
\operatorname{Stab}_{O^-(S)}(s_\infty).
$$

In the route-locked setting, the four sheets are the points of

$$
\Lambda_g\cong AG(2,2),
$$

and the terminal routing `12\mid34` selects one parallel class, equivalently one perfect matching of the four sheet labels.  The setwise stabiliser of that matching in `S_4` is

$$
S_2\wr S_2
\cong
D_8.
$$

### Corollary 5.1 — canonical incidence-level `D_8` nucleus

Every route-locked quartic core carries a canonically labelled minus-type four-space `S` together with a distinguished `D_8` coordinate subgroup: fix `s_\infty` and preserve the route matching on `\{s_0,s_1,s_2,s_3\}`.

This is the first canonical structural bridge between quartic curvature localisation and the `D_8` symmetry of the DDD atom.  It is not yet a proof that the physical DDD cocycle equals the curvature class.

## 6. The odd curvature quotient inside the nucleus

Let a terminal-side word be

$$
w=(w_i)_{i\in I}\in\mathbf F_2^I.
$$

Because the Gram matrix of the `s_i` is invertible, there is a unique vector

$$
\Psi(w)\in S
$$

such that

$$
b_X(\Psi(w),s_i)=w_i
\qquad(i\in I).
$$

If

$$
W=\sum_iw_i,
$$

then the inverse Gram matrix is again `K`, and therefore

$$
\boxed{
\Psi(w)
=
\sum_i(W+w_i)s_i.
}
$$

The affine functions

$$
AG(2,2)\longrightarrow\mathbf F_2
$$

are exactly the even-weight words in `\mathbf F_2^I`.  For even `w`, the formula becomes

$$
\Psi(w)=\sum_iw_is_i.
$$

Moreover,

$$
b_X(s_\infty,\sum_ia_is_i)
=
\sum_ia_i.
$$

Hence

$$
\boxed{
\Psi\bigl(\operatorname{Aff}(AG(2,2),\mathbf F_2)\bigr)
=
s_\infty^\perp.
}
$$

### Theorem 6.1 — curvature carrier

The parity quotient of terminal-side words is canonically identified with the one-dimensional quotient

$$
\boxed{
\mathbf F_2^I/
\operatorname{Aff}(AG(2,2),\mathbf F_2)
\cong
S/s_\infty^\perp.
}
$$

An odd terminal-side word represents the unique nonzero element of this quotient.

The route stabiliser `D_8` fixes `s_\infty`, preserves `s_\infty^\perp`, and therefore acts trivially on the one-dimensional quotient.  Thus the nonflat curvature bit now has a canonical incidence-level `D_8` carrier inside the terminal nucleus.

The remaining graph-level problem is to construct the physical comparison map to

$$
H^1(D_8,E_5)\cong\mathbf F_2
$$

and prove that the nonzero curvature carrier maps to the physical DDD class rather than merely having the same dimension and symmetry.

## 7. Projection of individual blocks

Let

$$
\operatorname{pr}_S:E_X\to S
$$

be orthogonal projection along `S^\perp`.  Fix `B\in\mathcal R_i` and define

$$
J(B)
=
\{j\in I\setminus\{i\}:e_j\in B\}.
$$

Because `|B|=4` and `e_i\notin B`,

$$
b_X(1_B,s_j)
=
\begin{cases}
1,&j\in J(B),\\
0,&j\notin J(B).
\end{cases}
$$

Therefore `\operatorname{pr}_S(1_B)=\Psi(1_{J(B)})`.

### Theorem 7.1 — terminal-block projection table

For a block `B\in\mathcal R_i`, its nucleus projection depends only on how many of the other three omitted points it contains:

$$
\begin{array}{c|c}
J(B)&\operatorname{pr}_S(1_B)\\
\hline
\varnothing&0\\
\{j\}&s_\infty+s_j\\
\{j,\ell\}&s_j+s_\ell\\
I\setminus\{i\}&s_i.
\end{array}
$$

The middle two cases are anisotropic roots of `S`; the last case is the singular sheet vector `s_i`.

Since the blocks of `\mathcal R_i` partition the three points `e_j` with `j\ne i`, every sheet has exactly one of three terminal-distribution types:

1. **concentrated:** `3`;
2. **split:** `2+1`;
3. **fully split:** `1+1+1`.

Thus every nonconcentrated sheet exposes two or three blocks carrying explicit anisotropic root projections in the canonical `E_5` nucleus.

## 8. Recursive peeling in the concentrated case

Assume now that every sheet is concentrated.  For each `i`, let `D_i\in\mathcal R_i` be the unique block containing all three points `e_j` with `j\ne i`.  Then

$$
D_i
=
\{e_j:j\ne i\}\cup\{y_i\}
$$

for one point

$$
y_i\in
Y
:=
X\setminus\{e_0,e_1,e_2,e_3\}.
$$

One has

$$
|Y|=4k-3=4(k-1)+1.
$$

Put

$$
\mathcal R_i'
=
\mathcal R_i\setminus\{D_i\}.
$$

### Theorem 8.1 — concentrated nucleus peeling

If `k\ge2`, then:

1. the four points `y_i` are pairwise distinct;
2. `\mathcal R_i'` partitions `Y\setminus\{y_i\}` into `k-1` four-element blocks;
3. all `4(k-1)` residual block vectors form a basis of `E_Y`;
4. the residual block vectors form a basis of `S^\perp` inside `E_X`;
5. consequently
   $$
   (Y,(\mathcal R_i'),(y_i))
   $$
   is a quartic witness design of level `k-1`.

#### Proof

The partition statement is immediate:

$$
\begin{aligned}
(X\setminus\{e_i\})\setminus D_i
&=
\bigl((E\setminus\{e_i\})\sqcup Y\bigr)
\setminus
\bigl((E\setminus\{e_i\})\sqcup\{y_i\}\bigr)\\
&=Y\setminus\{y_i\},
\end{aligned}
$$

where `E=\{e_0,e_1,e_2,e_3\}`.

Suppose `y_i=y_j` for distinct `i,j`.  Then

$$
\sum_{B\in\mathcal R_i'}1_B
=
1_Y+1_{y_i}
=
1_Y+1_{y_j}
=
\sum_{B\in\mathcal R_j'}1_B.
$$

Because `k\ge2`, both sums contain at least one original block vector.  Their equality gives a nontrivial linear relation among the global `4k` block basis, a contradiction.  Hence the `y_i` are distinct.

Every residual block contains none of the four points `e_j`, so Theorem 7.1 gives zero nucleus projection.  Thus all residual block vectors lie in `S^\perp`.  They remain independent because they are a subset of the original block basis.  Their number is

$$
4(k-1)=\dim S^\perp,
$$

so they form a basis of `S^\perp`.

They are also even vectors supported on `Y`; since

$$
\dim E_Y=|Y|-1=4(k-1),
$$

they form a basis of `E_Y`.  This proves the residual quartic-design axioms.  ∎

The distinguished blocks satisfy the exact triangular identity

$$
\boxed{
1_{D_i}+s_i
=
1_Y+1_{y_i}
=
\sum_{B\in\mathcal R_i'}1_B.
}
$$

Thus each distinguished block is its canonical nucleus vector `s_i` plus the whole-sheet sum of the smaller residual design.

For `k=1`, every sheet consists of the single block `X\setminus\{e_i\}`.  This is the five-point terminal nucleus itself; the four auxiliary points `y_i` coincide at the fifth point and there is no smaller quartic design.  It is the recursion base.

## 9. Inverse nucleus extension

The peeling theorem has an exact converse.

### Theorem 9.1 — canonical concentrated extension

Let

$$
(Y,(\mathcal R_i'),(y_i))
$$

be a quartic witness design of level `k-1\ge1`.  Adjoin four new points

$$
e_0,e_1,e_2,e_3
$$

and put

$$
X=Y\sqcup\{e_0,e_1,e_2,e_3\}.
$$

For each `i`, define

$$
D_i
=
\{e_j:j\ne i\}\cup\{y_i\},
$$

and

$$
\mathcal R_i
=
\mathcal R_i'\cup\{D_i\}.
$$

Then

$$
(X,(\mathcal R_i),(e_i))
$$

is a concentrated quartic witness design of level `k`.

#### Proof

The blocks in `\mathcal R_i` partition `X\setminus\{e_i\}` by construction.

Suppose

$$
\sum_i\alpha_i1_{D_i}
+
\sum_{B\in\bigcup_i\mathcal R_i'}\beta_B1_B
=0.
$$

At the coordinate `e_j`, only the blocks `D_i` with `i\ne j` contribute, so

$$
\sum_{i\ne j}\alpha_i=0
\qquad(j=0,1,2,3).
$$

The coefficient matrix is the invertible `K_4` Gram matrix from Theorem 3.1, hence every `\alpha_i=0`.  The remaining coefficients vanish by independence of the residual quartic design.  Therefore all `4k` new block vectors are independent.  Since they are even and

$$
\dim E_X=4k,
$$

they form a basis.  ∎

Hence concentrated quartic designs are exactly canonical terminal-nucleus extensions of smaller quartic designs, with the five-point core as the base case.

## 10. Structural consequence for unbounded cores

### Corollary 10.1 — split-or-peel dichotomy

Every quartic witness design satisfies exactly one of the following.

1. **Root-exposed split.**  Some sheet has terminal distribution `2+1` or `1+1+1`.  That sheet contains at least two blocks whose projections to the canonical nucleus are anisotropic `E_5` roots.
2. **Nucleus recursion.**  Every sheet is concentrated.  If `k=1`, the design is the five-point nucleus.  If `k\ge2`, the nucleus peels off and leaves a quartic witness design of level `k-1`.

Therefore every abstract unbounded quartic design is obtained by iterating canonical `O^-(4,2)` nucleus extensions until one reaches either:

- the five-point nucleus; or
- a first root-exposed split design.

The explicit quartic ladder is the special case in which the recursion remains concentrated at every level.

## 11. Updated physical frontier

The abstract quartic branch is no longer an undifferentiated unbounded design problem.  The exact next physical theorem has two branches.

### Root-exposed branch

Use the two or three anisotropic terminal-block projections in a split sheet to force one of:

- a bounded DDD/Petersen factor;
- a two-edge transition interval;
- a transition-matroid split;
- a smaller cyclic separator;
- a ten-state four-pole transfer.

### Concentrated branch

Show that the algebraic peeling

$$
E_X=S\perp E_Y
$$

is respected by the ribbon rotations, quotient Kempe cycle systems, endpoint selector labels, and terminal route.  Then either:

- the canonical `D_8` nucleus is composition-safe and can be removed as a bounded factor; or
- failure of descent is itself a bounded crossed interface exposing the physical DDD class.

The remaining obstruction is therefore no longer pure incidence rank.  It is the compatibility of a canonical recursive `O^-(4,2)` nucleus decomposition with the physical transition and gluing semantics.

## 12. Reliability boundary

Proved here:

- every quartic design contains a canonical nondegenerate minus-type four-space;
- its five singular points and ten anisotropic vectors form the intrinsic `E_5/K_5` geometry;
- route matching gives a canonical incidence-level `D_8` stabiliser;
- the odd terminal curvature quotient is canonically `S/s_\infty^\perp`;
- individual block projections are determined by terminal-point distribution;
- every all-concentrated design of level `k\ge2` peels to a level `k-1` quartic design;
- concentrated extension is the exact inverse construction;
- every abstract quartic core satisfies the split-or-peel dichotomy.

Not proved here:

- that a root-exposed split block gives a physical graph reduction;
- that nucleus peeling preserves the physical ribbon/Kempe enrichment;
- that the incidence-level `D_8` curvature carrier equals the physical DDD cohomology class;
- singleton or two-edge composition;
- defect-forest pruning or the global five-support theorem.

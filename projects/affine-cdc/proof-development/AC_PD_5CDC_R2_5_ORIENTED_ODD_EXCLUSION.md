## 1. The distinguished physical block

Let the original two-vertex cap have ordered matching

\[
M_i=X_i\mid Y_i.
\]

The two cap vertices distinguish the two blocks.  Fix `X_i` as the positive
block.

This datum is retained in every complete contextual state:

- the four outer incidences are fixed and ordered;
- the cap block is a labelled partition, not an unordered matching orbit;
- local NNIs, support corrections and relative source movies fix those outer
  incidence labels;
- a move which changes the physical route is an accepted cap/profile exit.

Hence throughout a nonexit fixed-route descendant context the same positive
block `X_i` remains distinguished.

## 2. Oriented full-channel lock

At equality or DDD boundary, each relevant support-pair channel contains the
two marked reconnection edges in one component.  Cutting that component at the
marked edges yields the original cap matching `M_i` in its fixed cyclic order.

Thus the lock records more than same-component connectivity: every channel
returns the distinguished positive block to itself.

For the DDD sector let `D` be the physical transport subgroup generated by
these channel passages.  If the chosen crossed route is `X`, block exchange is
the character `kappa`; if it is `Y`, block exchange is `kappa+chi`.  Physical
admissibility gives

\[
D\le\ker\nu,
\]

where `nu` is the relevant block-exchange character.  Therefore

\[
\boxed{\nu(g)=0\quad(g\in D).}
\]

This kernel statement applies to every physical transport segment whose pivot
and outer cap route close; it does not wait for the complete enriched state to
repeat.

## 3. Resolution parity

A forced DDD state is one Petersen edge

\[
F=\{s,t\}
\]

with crossed root-resolution fibre `{s,t}`.  Traversing the switch from pivot
`s` to pivot `t` exchanges the two shore-demand sheets:

\[
s\longleftrightarrow t.
\]

Hence a pivot segment of length `m` has resolution transport

\[
\operatorname{Hol}_{\mathrm{res}}=m\pmod2.
\]

The forced-backbone crossed-route calibration identifies this resolution
exchange with the physical oriented-block exchange character `nu`.  Constant
pivot runs contribute no exchange because their unique nonpivot section is
root-valued; a diagrammatic Type-T bigon contributes two exchanges and hence
zero.

### Lemma 3.1 — physical parity identity

For every forced-backbone segment inside the fixed oriented lock,

\[
\boxed{\nu(\gamma)=|\gamma|\pmod2.}
\]

The equality is an endpoint action on the actual crossed route sheet.  It is
not a claim that parity detects the independent affine `D_8` cohomology class.

## 4. Pivot-closed physical subsegments

Let

\[
\gamma=s_0,F_1,s_1,\ldots,F_m,s_m
\]

be a physical forced-backbone subsegment with

\[
s_m=s_0.
\]

Because the pivot and fixed cap route close, the complete transport along
`gamma` is an element of the physical subgroup `D`.  Therefore

\[
\nu(\gamma)=0.
\]

By Lemma 3.1,

\[
m\equiv0\pmod2.
\]

### Theorem 4.1 — no odd pivot-closed physical segment

Every pivot-closed forced-backbone segment in the oriented inverse-dipole lock
has even length.

This conclusion requires neither equality of all side attachments at the two
endpoints nor prior recurrence of the complete state.  Pivot closure and the
fixed oriented cap block suffice.

## 5. Double laps do not evade the theorem

An abstract odd Petersen cycle lifts to a path exchanging the two sheets of the
canonical resolution double cover.  Traversing the base cycle twice would
return abstractly.

Physically, however, after the first lap:

- the pivot has already returned;
- the positive cap block has been exchanged with its antipode.

The first lap is therefore itself an odd pivot-closed physical segment and is
forbidden by Theorem 4.1.  The antipodal endpoint is not a second admissible
state of the same oriented lock.

### Corollary 5.1

No recurrent physical track can realise two or more laps of an odd Petersen
core.  The double-cover loophole is absent.

## 1. The DDD stabiliser

Fix a perfect matching of a four-set

\[
M=\{r_1,r_2\},
\]

where, after relabelling,

\[
r_1=12,
\qquad
r_2=34.
\]

Let

\[
c=r_1+r_2=11110
\]

be the co-root corresponding to the omitted fifth support.

The setwise stabiliser of `M` in `S_4` is

\[
D_8=(C_2\times C_2)\rtimes C_2.
\]

Choose generators

\[
a=(12),
\qquad
b=(34),
\qquad
p=(13)(24).
\]

Then

- `a` flips the endpoints of `r_1`;
- `b` flips the endpoints of `r_2`;
- `p` exchanges `r_1` and `r_2`;
- `pap=b`.

Every element has a unique form

\[
g=a^{\epsilon_1}b^{\epsilon_2}p^\sigma,
\qquad
\epsilon_1,\epsilon_2,\sigma\in\mathbf F_2.
\]

On the two-dimensional subspace

\[
L=\langle r_1,r_2\rangle,
\]

the base group generated by `a,b` acts trivially and `p` exchanges `r_1,r_2`.

## 2. The explicit cocycle

Define

\[
\alpha:D_8\longrightarrow E_5
\]

by

\[
\boxed{
\alpha(a^{\epsilon_1}b^{\epsilon_2}p^\sigma)
=\epsilon_1r_2+\epsilon_2r_1.
}
\]

Equivalently,

\[
\alpha(a)=r_2,
\qquad
\alpha(b)=r_1,
\qquad
\alpha(p)=0.
\]

### Theorem 2.1 — cocycle identity

\[
\boxed{
\alpha(gh)=\alpha(g)+g\alpha(h)
\qquad(g,h\in D_8).
}
\]

### Proof

Write the semidirect product coordinates as `(v,sigma)` with

\[
v=(\epsilon_1,\epsilon_2)\in\mathbf F_2^2.
\]

Let `J` be the equivariant map

\[
J(\epsilon_1,\epsilon_2)
=\epsilon_1r_2+\epsilon_2r_1.
\]

The nontrivial `C_2` swaps the two coordinates of `v` and simultaneously swaps `r_1,r_2`, so `J` is equivariant. The group law is

\[
(v,\sigma)(w,\tau)
=(v+\sigma w,\sigma+\tau).
\]

Therefore

\[
\alpha((v,\sigma)(w,\tau))
=Jv+J(\sigma w)
=\alpha(v,\sigma)+(v,\sigma)\alpha(w,\tau).
\]

## 3. Nontriviality

### Theorem 3.1

The cocycle `alpha` is not a coboundary.

### Proof

Suppose there were `y in E_5` with

\[
\alpha(g)=y+gy
\qquad(g\in D_8).
\]

Since `alpha(p)=0`, the vector `y` would be fixed by

\[
p=(13)(24).
\]

Thus its first four coordinates have the form

\[
(x_1,x_2,x_1,x_2).
\]

But

\[
y+ay
\]

for `a=(12)` is supported on the first pair `12`, and hence lies in `{0,r_1}`. It cannot equal

\[
\alpha(a)=r_2.
\]

Contradiction.

Since the active corpus gives

\[
H^1(D_8,E_5)\cong\mathbf F_2,
\]

the class of `alpha` is its unique nonzero element.

## 4. The sheet-swap character is a coboundary

Let

\[
\chi:D_8\longrightarrow\mathbf F_2
\]

be the quotient character recording whether `g` exchanges the two matching edges `r_1,r_2`; in the chosen coordinates,

\[
\chi(a^{\epsilon_1}b^{\epsilon_2}p^\sigma)=\sigma.
\]

The co-root-valued map

\[
\beta(g)=\chi(g)c
\]

is a cocycle because `c` is `D_8`-fixed.

### Theorem 4.1 — gauge triviality of sheet exchange

\[
\boxed{
\beta(g)=r_1+gr_1.
}
\]

Hence

\[
\boxed{[\beta]=0\in H^1(D_8,E_5).}
\]

### Proof

If `chi(g)=0`, then `g` preserves `r_1` setwise, so `r_1+gr_1=0`. If `chi(g)=1`, then `g r_1=r_2`, so

\[
r_1+gr_1=r_1+r_2=c.
\]

## 5. Mechanistic interpretation

The two natural binary quantities in the DDD stabiliser have different meanings.

### Sheet exchange

The quotient

\[
D_8/V_4\cong C_2
\]

records whether the two disjoint-root sheets are exchanged. Its natural co-root translation `chi c` is a change of affine origin and is therefore gauge-trivial.

### Internal twist

The nontrivial cohomology class is represented by `alpha`:

- flipping the endpoints inside `r_1` contributes the **opposite root** `r_2`;
- flipping the endpoints inside `r_2` contributes `r_1`;
- exchanging the two sheets contributes no class by itself.

Thus the genuine DDD affine obstruction is an internal-twist defect, not the bare sheet-swap bit.

## 6. Abelian characters and a scalar shadow

The internal-flip parity

\[
\kappa(a^{\epsilon_1}b^{\epsilon_2}p^\sigma)
=\epsilon_1+\epsilon_2
\]

is invariant under the pair swap and defines a character of `D_8`. It is distinct from the sheet-swap character `chi`.

The vector cocycle `alpha` refines `kappa`: pairing `alpha` with any `D_8`-compatible covector taking value one on both `r_1,r_2` recovers `kappa`.

A canonical comparison of this scalar shadow with the full-channel curvature bit remains to be constructed.

## 7. Consequences for overlap transport

For an octahedral overlap return, the cumulative physical transport

\[
\pi_\gamma\in D_8
\]

has coordinates

\[
(\epsilon_1,\epsilon_2;\sigma).
\]

The finite diagnostics are therefore:

1. `sigma`: sheet exchange, gauge-trivial as an `E_5` cocycle;
2. `epsilon_1+epsilon_2`: internal-twist parity;
3. `alpha(pi_gamma)=epsilon_1r_2+epsilon_2r_1`: the explicit vector representative of the exceptional DDD class.

This corrects the provisional hypothesis that the return quotient `D_8/V_4` itself should be the DDD cohomology class.

## 8. Remaining physical frontier

The explicit group cocycle does not yet show that a given overlap strip carries the affine translation `alpha(pi_gamma)`. The physical lifted loop has its own translation chain, and one must construct a canonical comparison map.

### Target 8.1 — transport-to-affine chain map

For an overlap word with cumulative transport `pi_gamma`, express the difference between transported root frames and the actual endpoint witness as an `E_5` chain `z_gamma`, and prove that its cohomology class is either zero or the pullback of `[alpha]`.

### Target 8.2 — localisation

If the pulled-back class is nonzero, localise the first internal-twist return to a bounded DDD blossom, common cut, or defect-forest unit. If it is zero, construct a gauge/serial reduction or profile escape.

### Target 8.3 — curvature comparison

Construct, or disprove the existence of, a canonical chain map sending `[alpha]` to the common-cut/full-channel curvature obstruction. Equality by dimension counting is not sufficient.

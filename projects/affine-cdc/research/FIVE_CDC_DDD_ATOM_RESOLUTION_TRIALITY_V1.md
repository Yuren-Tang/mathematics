# DDD atoms and the three-resolution triality

**Parents:** `FIVE_CDC_DEFECT_MINIMAL_FOREST_PETERSEN_TRANSPORT_V1.md`, `FIVE_CDC_BBD_GLOBAL_AFFINE_HOLONOMY_K6_DEFECT_STRIP_V1.md`

## 1. Purpose

A defect-minimal BBD flow has an induced defect forest.  Its co-root leaves are `K_6` one-factor vertices.  The smallest co-root component consists of one co-root edge joining two such leaves.

This packet proves that the nondegenerate two-vertex co-root component is not an accidental local obstruction.  It is canonically equivalent to one DDD boundary state, and its three possible pairings of the four terminals are exactly the three edges of that `K_6` one-factor.

Equivalently:

\[
\boxed{
\text{DDD one-factor}
=
\text{one co-root resolution plus two root resolutions}.
}
\]

This identifies the DDD state space as the intrinsic resolution space of the smallest residual BBD defect.

No claim is made that changing the terminal pairing is a valid replacement in the original graph without an additional composition theorem.

## 2. The two-vertex co-root atom

Fix a support index `i` and write

\[
q_i=\mathbf1+e_i.
\]

Let

\[
Q=[5]\setminus\{i\}.
\]

There are three perfect matchings of the complete graph on `Q`.  Choose two distinct matchings

\[
M_0,\qquad M_1.
\]

Construct a two-vertex four-pole:

- the two internal vertices are joined by an edge labelled `q_i`;
- the two terminal roots at the left vertex are the two edges of `M_0`;
- the two terminal roots at the right vertex are the two edges of `M_1`.

At each endpoint, the two matching roots sum to `q_i`, so cubic conservation holds.

This is called the **co-root atom**

\[
A(i;M_0,M_1).
\]

The case `M_0=M_1` is degenerate: the equal boundary roots can be paired directly.  The nondegenerate atom has `M_0 != M_1`.

## 3. The omitted matching and the DDD state

Let `M_*` be the third perfect matching of `K_Q`:

\[
\{M_0,M_1,M_*\}
=
\{\text{the three perfect matchings of }K_Q\}.
\]

Write

\[
M_*=\{X,Y\},
\]

where `X,Y` are disjoint roots.

Define

\[
\boxed{
F_*(A)=\{\infty i,X,Y\}.
}
\]

This is a perfect matching of `K_6`, hence one of the fifteen DDD/one-factor states.

### Theorem 3.1 — atom-state bijection

The map

\[
A(i;M_0,M_1)\longmapsto F_*(A)
\]

is an `S_5`-equivariant bijection between:

- the fifteen unordered nondegenerate co-root atoms;
- the fifteen perfect matchings of `K_6`.

#### Proof

For each `i`, the three choices of an unordered pair of distinct matchings are in bijection with the omitted third matching.  Thus there are

\[
5\cdot3=15
\]

atoms, and the construction is visibly equivariant under support permutations.  Conversely, a one-factor `\{\infty i,X,Y\}` determines the omitted matching `\{X,Y\}`, and hence the other two matchings used at the atom endpoints. ∎

The stabiliser of an atom is therefore the stabiliser of its DDD state:

\[
\boxed{
\operatorname{Stab}_{S_5}(A)
=
\operatorname{Stab}_{S_5}(F_*(A))
\cong D_8.
}
\]

This is exactly the dihedral group generated by the physical DDD monodromies.

## 4. Three terminal pairings

The four boundary roots of a nondegenerate atom are the four edges of `K_Q` outside the omitted matching `M_*`.  They form a four-cycle.

Relabel

\[
Q=\{a,b,c,d\}
\]

so that

\[
M_0=\{ab,cd\},
\qquad
M_1=\{ac,bd\},
\qquad
M_*=\{ad,bc\}.
\]

There are exactly three partitions of the four terminal roots into two unordered pairs.  For a chosen pair of terminal roots `U,V`, the internal label forced by cubic conservation is

\[
U+V.
\]

### Theorem 4.1 — resolution triality

The three possible terminal pairings force the three internal labels

\[
\boxed{
q_i,\qquad e_a+e_d,\qquad e_b+e_c.
}
\]

Equivalently, the set of internal labels is exactly

\[
\boxed{
\{\infty i,ad,bc\}=F_*(A).
}
\]

#### Proof

1. The original endpoint pairing is
   \[
   \{ab,cd\}\mid\{ac,bd\}.
   \]
   Each pair sums to the complement vector `q_i`.

2. One crossed pairing is
   \[
   \{ab,bd\}\mid\{ac,cd\}.
   \]
   Both pairs sum to `ad`.

3. The other crossed pairing is
   \[
   \{ab,ac\}\mid\{cd,bd\}.
   \]
   Both pairs sum to `bc`. ∎

Thus one DDD state simultaneously records:

- the current co-root realisation of the atom;
- the first root-valued resolution;
- the second root-valued resolution.

Exact Wolfram enumeration verifies the identity for all fifteen atoms.

## 5. Four-terminal routing interpretation

A set of four terminals has exactly three pairings.  The resolution triality identifies those three routing colours with the three edges of the one-factor `F_*(A)`.

Hence:

\[
\boxed{
\begin{array}{c}
\text{three }AG(2,2)\text{ terminal directions}\\
=\\
\text{three local atom resolutions}\\
=\\
\text{three edges of one DDD }K_6\text{ one-factor}.
\end{array}
}
\]

This is the local point where the original four-terminal routing geometry, the DDD triangle, and the co-root defect forest become literally the same object.

## 6. Petersen incidence form

Under the Petersen model, the DDD state

\[
F_*(A)=\{\infty i,X,Y\}
\]

corresponds to the Petersen edge

\[
XY.
\]

The two root-valued resolutions have internal labels `X` and `Y`, the two endpoints of that Petersen edge.  The co-root resolution is the remaining pair `\infty i`.

Therefore:

\[
\boxed{
\text{co-root atom}=\text{Petersen edge},
\qquad
\text{root resolution}=\text{choice of one endpoint}.
}
\]

Together with the previous packet:

- a zero equality leaf carries a Petersen vertex label;
- a nondegenerate co-root atom carries a Petersen edge label;
- a co-root strip transports Petersen edges through `L(Petersen)`;
- the DDD group is the edge stabiliser.

The residual forest is therefore governed by Petersen incidence, not merely by a list of fifteen states.

## 7. What is and is not reduced

The atom cannot be made root-valued while keeping the same incidence of its four terminals: the original endpoint pairs necessarily force the co-root `q_i`.

The two root resolutions require changing the pairing of the four terminal semiedges.  Therefore the theorem does **not** by itself replace the atom inside the original graph.

A valid global reduction must prove that at least one crossed resolution:

1. preserves the relevant enriched boundary semantics;
2. corresponds to a permitted Whitney/dot-product/four-pole operation;
3. or creates a strictly smaller separator or orbit-escape state.

This trust boundary is essential.  The triality identifies the exact two candidate resolutions; it does not assume that either one transfers a cycle double cover back to the original incidence graph.

## 8. Updated pruning target

For every leaf-to-leaf one-edge co-root component, the obstruction has become a finite choice:

\[
\boxed{
\text{retain the co-root DDD edge state}
\quad\text{or select one of its two Petersen endpoints as a root resolution}.
}
\]

The next composition theorem should show:

\[
\boxed{
\begin{array}{c}
\text{both root resolutions blocked}\\
\Downarrow\\
\text{the opposite shore realises the DDD stabiliser constraint}\\
\Downarrow\\
\text{smaller four-pole factor, profile escape, or the unique }D_8\text{ affine class}.
\end{array}
}
\]

This is the first bounded atom-level interface for defect-forest pruning.

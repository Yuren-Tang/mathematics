## 1. Setting

After the rank-two Tait-escape theorem, a universally locked atom can survive only when its challenge quotient

\[
c:E(Q)\to E_4\setminus\{0\}
\cong F_2^3\setminus\{0\}
\]

has full rank three.

All four terminal values are

\[
g=1111,
\]

and all four scalar coordinate flows pair the terminals as

\[
12\mid34.
\]

Let

\[
E_g=\{e:c(e)=g\}.
\]

Choosing side bits on the two terminal paths and on every scalar closed cycle gives a curvature vector

\[
\omega\in F_2^{E_g}.
\]

Changing the side bit of a closed scalar component `C` adds

\[
1_{C\cap E_g}
\]

to `omega`.  Hence the curvature class is

\[
\Omega(c)
\in
F_2^{E_g}/W,
\]

where

\[
W=
\left\langle
1_{C\cap E_g}:
C\text{ a closed component of one scalar sheet}
\right\rangle.
\]

The flat case `Omega=0` is equivalent to the affine-potential system proved in the parent packet.  This packet gives an exact dual form of the nonflat case.

## 2. Dual witness

### Theorem 2.1 — curvature dual certificate

The following are equivalent:

1. `Omega(c) != 0`;
2. there exists
   \[
   \eta\in F_2^{E_g}
   \]
   such that
   \[
   \langle\eta,\omega\rangle=1
   \]
   and
   \[
   \langle\eta,1_{C\cap E_g}\rangle=0
   \]
   for every closed scalar component `C`.

#### Proof

The class of `omega` is nonzero in the quotient `F_2^{E_g}/W` exactly when some linear functional on the quotient evaluates nontrivially on it.  Pulling that functional back to `F_2^{E_g}` gives `eta`, which annihilates `W` and pairs oddly with `omega`. ∎

We identify `eta` with its support, a subset

\[
\eta\subseteq E_g.
\]

## 3. The same edge set is a cut in all four sheets

For coordinate `j`, let

\[
H_j
\]

be the scalar relative even subgraph.  It is a disjoint union of:

- the terminal path joining `1,2`;
- the terminal path joining `3,4`;
- closed cycles.

Every `g`-edge lies in every `H_j`, so `eta` is an edge subset of all four sheets.

### Theorem 3.1 — common-cut theorem

For every scalar sheet `H_j`, the set `eta` is a cut vector:

\[
\boxed{
\eta=\delta_{H_j}h_j
}
\]

for some binary vertex potential

\[
h_j:V(H_j)\to F_2.
\]

#### Proof

The cycle space of `H_j` is generated by its closed cycle components.  By Theorem 2.1, `eta` has even intersection with every such component.  Hence `eta` is orthogonal to the cycle space of `H_j`, and therefore lies in its cut space. ∎

Thus a curved atom has one physical set of `g`-edges that is simultaneously a cut in all four locked scalar sheets.

## 4. Boundary parity identity

Fix the side convention:

- the `12` terminal path in every sheet has side `0`;
- the `34` terminal path has side `1`.

For each sheet choose `h_j` with

\[
\delta h_j=\eta.
\]

### Theorem 4.1 — odd terminal word

\[
\boxed{
\langle\eta,\omega\rangle
=
\sum_{j=1}^4
\bigl(h_j(t_3)+h_j(t_4)\bigr).
}
\]

Consequently, in the nonflat case the four-bit terminal word

\[
b(\eta)=
\bigl(
 h_1(t_3)+h_1(t_4),
 \ldots,
 h_4(t_3)+h_4(t_4)
\bigr)
\]

has odd parity:

\[
\boxed{
\sum_j b_j(\eta)=1.
}
\]

#### Proof

Evaluate the pairing sheet by sheet.  On a closed scalar component, `eta` has even size, so its contribution is zero.  On the `12` path, the side label is zero.  On the `34` path, the side label is one, and the parity of `eta` on that path is exactly the endpoint difference `h_j(t_3)+h_j(t_4)`.  Summing over the four sheets gives the identity. ∎

\[

\[
\boxed{

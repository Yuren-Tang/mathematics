**Status:** exact exhaustive finite certificate for inherited-flow, rather than mere global-flow, flexibility. The Kempe reconfiguration graph of labelled `R_5` root flows can have several components, and a marked edge pair may be `B`-attainable globally while inaccessible from the inherited flow's component. This occurs on the triangular prism and `K_(3,3)`. In contrast, the Petersen graph has exactly two Kempe components, each of size `3000`, and every one of its `105` edge pairs reaches the distinct-intersecting `B` orbit inside both components; the maximum required distance is three component switches. ## 1. Exact reconfiguration model

Let `G` be a labelled cubic graph. A labelled root flow is

\[
\lambda:E(G)\to R_5
\]

such that the three incident roots at every vertex sum to zero.

For one support pair

\[
s=ab,
\]

the channel graph is

\[
A_s(\lambda)=F_a\triangle F_b.
\]

Every connected component of `A_s(lambda)` is a circuit. Switching one complete component replaces

\[
\lambda(e)\longmapsto\lambda(e)+s
\]

on that circuit and preserves root-valuedness.

Define the **Kempe reconfiguration graph**

\[
\mathcal K(G)
\]

by:

- vertices: all labelled root flows on `G`;
- edges: one support-pair component switch.

For marked graph edges `e,f`, let

\[
\mathcal B_{e,f}
=
\{\lambda:\lambda(e),\lambda(f)\text{ are distinct and intersecting}\}.
\]

The inherited-flow question is not merely whether `B_(e,f)` is nonempty. It is whether every component of `K(G)` meets `B_(e,f)`.

All enumerations below are complete. Root flows were generated by the exact local cubic constraint, and every Kempe edge was reconstructed from all ten support-pair channel graphs.

- six require at most one Kempe switch.

Global two-edge attainability does not imply inherited-flow attainability even on a six-vertex cubic graph.

## 4. `K_(3,3)`

Exact data:

\[
\boxed{
|V(\mathcal K(K_{3,3}))|=840,
\qquad
|E(\mathcal K(K_{3,3}))|=4140.
}

There are eight reconfiguration components:

\[
\boxed{
6\times120
+
2\times60.
}
\]

Every unordered edge pair is globally `B`-attainable, but componentwise behaviour splits exactly by incidence.

### Adjacent edge pairs

There are eighteen adjacent pairs. They are already in `B` for every root flow:

\[
\boxed{
8/8\text{ components meet }\mathcal B_{e,f},
\qquad
\text{distance }0.
}
\]

### Disjoint edge pairs

There are eighteen disjoint pairs. For every such pair:

\[
\boxed{
4/8\text{ components meet }\mathcal B_{e,f},
\qquad
4/8\text{ components do not}.}
\]

Thus `K_(3,3)` gives the cleanest counterexample to the implication

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal B_{e,f}\ne\varnothing
\Longrightarrow
\text{every inherited flow can reach }\mathcal B_{e,f}.
}
\]

`K_(3,3)` has cyclic four-edge cuts, so this obstruction is compatible with the need for the accepted small-cut reduction before a prime theorem.

## 5. Petersen graph

Use the standard labelled Petersen graph with fifteen edges. Exact flow enumeration gives

\[
\boxed{6000}
\]

labelled root flows.

Its Kempe graph has

\[
\boxed{37200}
\]

reconfiguration edges and exactly two connected components:

\[
\boxed{3000,\ 3000.}
\]

### Theorem 5.1 — componentwise two-edge flexibility on Petersen

For every unordered pair of Petersen edges `e,f`, both Kempe components meet

\[
\mathcal B_{e,f}.
\]

Equivalently, from every inherited root flow and for every marked edge pair, a finite sequence of support-pair component switches reaches a flow in which the two marked roots are distinct and intersecting.

### Exact distance bound

There are

\[
\binom{15}{2}=105
\]

unordered edge pairs.

- For the thirty adjacent graph-edge pairs, every root flow already lies in `B`:
  \[
  \boxed{d_{\max}=0.}
  \]
- For the seventy-five nonadjacent pairs, multi-source distance to `B_(e,f)` in `K(P)` satisfies
  \[
  \boxed{d_{\max}=3.}
  \]

Thus Petersen has the uniform inherited-flow bound

\[
\boxed{
\forall\lambda,e,f\quad
\operatorname{dist}_{\mathcal K(P)}
(\lambda,\mathcal B_{e,f})\le3.
}
\]

The two global Kempe components are not obstructions to any two-edge weld condition.

## 7. Structural interpretation

The obstruction is not the number of Kempe components by itself:

- Petersen has two components but both are fully two-edge flexible;
- `K_(3,3)` has eight components, and disjoint pairs are flexible in only half;
- the prism has one-sided component traps even beyond its globally bad pairs.

The decisive graph-side input appears to be the prime source geometry after:

- triangle contraction / bounded base disposal;
- cyclic three- and four-cut reduction;
- retention of the full inherited flow rather than arbitrary reselection.

This matches the PDL source target and explains why a generic coefficient or profile theorem failed.

## 8. Revised prime target

### Target 8.1 — Kempe-component two-edge flexibility

Let `G` be a connected simple triangle-free cubic graph in the prime branch after all accepted cyclic cuts of size at most four are removed. Let

\[
\lambda:E(G)\to R_5
\]

be any inherited root flow, and let `e,f` be two distinguished nonloop edges.

Prove one of:

1. within the Kempe component of `lambda`, some flow `lambda'` satisfies
   \[
   (\lambda'(e),\lambda'(f))\in B;
   \]
2. the channel full lock exposes an accepted separator or bounded source configuration;
3. a route/profile event solves the outer terminal before the weld condition is needed;
4. a strict marked contextual reduction produces a smaller instance.

A stronger quantitative conjecture, suggested by Petersen but not required, is a bounded switch distance depending only on the local marked-lock geometry.

## 9. Relation to inherited marked return

The canonical cancellation supplies one inherited lower-order flow already satisfying the weld `B` relation. During lower-order cap return, the first loss of `B` gives one zero/co-root atom on a shorter prefix.

Target 8.1 is relevant at that first exit:

- it asks whether the inherited component contains a route back to `B`;
- it does not discard the constructing flow;
- it does not infer simultaneous profile intersection from separate existence;
- separator and route exits remain explicit.

It is therefore strictly closer to the required continuation-normalized local rank than generic two-edge flow selection.

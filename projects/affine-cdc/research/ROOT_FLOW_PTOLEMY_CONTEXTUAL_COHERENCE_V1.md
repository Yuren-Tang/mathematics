## 1. Triangle pseudocomplex and ordinary flip blocks

Let

\[
x:E(G)\to R_5
\]

be a root-valued flow. The triangle-complex theorem associates to every source vertex one support-labelled triangle and glues equal root-labelled sides. Denote the resulting finite triangle pseudocomplex by

\[
\mathfrak T_x.
\]

Split every singular complex vertex according to the connected components of its link. This operation:

1. does not change the dual cubic graph;
2. does not change any triangle, side label, or root flow;
3. produces a compact surface, possibly disconnected or nonorientable, with marked vertices and boundary arcs;
4. turns every ordinary pair of adjacent distinct source triangles into an ordinary quadrilateral diagonal.

A root-valued `2--2` Pachner NNI is exactly the diagonal flip in that quadrilateral.

### Degenerate local configurations

If the two triangles do not form an ordinary embedded quadrilateral after link splitting, then one of the following occurs:

- equal triangles, giving the strict `2--0` cancellation;
- a repeated side or self-folded local cell;
- a loop or parallel-edge neighbourhood in the dual graph;
- a two-/three-/four-edge separator or bounded appendage.

These are already strict vertex descent or established category exits. Therefore a **same-order prime block** may be assumed to consist entirely of ordinary diagonal flips on one fixed marked compact surface.

## 2. Standard Ptolemy presentation

Fix one marked surface `(S,V)` and one connected component of its triangulation/fatgraph complex.

### External Theorem 2.1 — Ptolemy coherence

The path groupoid generated by elementary diagonal flips has the presentation:

1. **involutivity**
   \[
   F_eF_{e'}=1,
   \]
   where `e'` is the new diagonal created by flipping `e`;

2. **far commutativity**
   \[
   F_eF_f=F_fF_e
   \]
   when the two quadrilaterals have disjoint triangle supports;

3. **pentagon**
   the two flip paths across one five-triangle/five-leaf local configuration are identified by the standard five-flip relation.

Equivalently, every closed flip history is reducible to the empty history by finitely many applications of these three relations.

### Exceptional surfaces

The low-complexity exceptional triangulation complexes are disks, annuli, punctured digons, or sphere-like finite cases with boundedly many arcs. In the present four-port programme they are already contained in:

- the zero-vertex direct-pairing terminal;
- the two-vertex theta/parallel case;
- the six-vertex identity annulus;
- bounded two-/three-/four-port degeneration;
- separator gluing.

Thus no unbounded prime branch lies outside the standard presentation.

## 3. Contextual root states

A **contextual state** consists of:

1. one finite cubic source graph or multipole of fixed order;
2. one root-valued `E_5` flow on every ordinary edge;
3. the ordered outer four-port incidences and the distinguished cap route;
4. optionally one normalized first-failure edge atom, with value zero or one co-root;
5. the local pairing/resolution sheet of that atom.

A contextual move is one of:

- a root Pachner flip;
- the inverse of a root Pachner flip;
- a strict equal-face cancellation or its attempted inverse;
- relocation/normalization of one first-failure atom through a critical overlap.

At fixed source order, cancellations and inverse insertions are omitted. They belong to the strict-order layer. Hence a fixed-order contextual history is built from root flips and at most one atom token.

## 4. Lift of the involutivity relation

A root Pachner flip between two root topologies is unique and reversible. Flipping the new diagonal back restores:

- the same labelled source graph;
- the same four local exterior roots;
- the same old central root;
- the same outer route data.

### Theorem 4.1 — root involutivity

Every consecutive flip/inverse-flip pair deletes exactly from a contextual history.

If an atom lies outside the two-vertex support, deletion is literal. If the atom lies on the shared support, the critical-overlap table reduces the pair first to one normalized atom and then to the same state. No monodromy remains.

---

## 5. Lift of far commutativity

For two disjoint two-vertex supports, the source replacements commute on the nose. The resulting graph has the same exterior incidence identifications and the same root labels in either order.

The critical-overlap theorem adds:

- a first-failure atom disjoint from both supports is unchanged;
- an atom meeting one support is affected only by that move;
- no second atom is created.

### Theorem 5.1 — contextual square

Every Ptolemy commutativity square lifts to a commutative square of contextual root states, or one side exits through an already established separator/category branch.

Thus a disjoint-square relation contributes no scheduling monodromy.

## 6. Lift of the pentagon relation

Two adjacent flip supports form one five-leaf local tree. The five-leaf root-interval theorem gives the exact root-valid subset of every Ptolemy pentagon.

For a fixed ordered conserved five-root boundary, root-valued topologies on the pentagon form one cyclic interval of size

\[
0,1,2,3,5.
\]

### Case 6.1 — both comparison paths are root-valued

If the short path has a singular intermediate state but its two branch endpoints are root-valued, the opposite three-step path is entirely root-valued. This is the exact root-compatible pentagon commutation.

### Case 6.2 — only one side remains root-valued

The valid interval has one endpoint on the attempted comparison path. The first failure moves monotonically to that endpoint and remains one normalized zero/co-root atom. It cannot disappear and later reappear inside the same pentagon.

### Case 6.3 — the complete pentagon is root-valued

The five topologies form the support-five-cycle sector. By the exact Petersen dictionary, the five edges are DDD switch states and a completed lap has odd resolution parity. An oriented cap lock forbids the resulting shore exchange. Therefore a full root-valued pentagon relation cannot be a closed fixed-route contextual loop; it forces route escape or another established exit.

### Theorem 6.1 — contextual pentagon

Every Ptolemy pentagon has exactly one of:

1. a fully root-valued comparison between its two paths;
2. one nonbranching normalized atom relocated to one interval endpoint;
3. route/profile escape from the oriented fixed-route sector;
4. separator/category exit.

No pentagon creates two atoms or a new coefficient state.

## 7. Closed local root circuits

The complete five-leaf classification strengthens the one-pentagon analysis. Among all fifteen five-leaf topologies, every nonempty root sector is exactly:

\[
C_5
\quad\text{or}\quad
C_6.
\]

The circuit-disposition theorem proves:

- `C5` is the odd Petersen/DDD resolution cycle and cannot close in an oriented cap lock;
- `C6` returns to the identical labelled graph, root flow, and resolution sheet, so one complete lap deletes from a length-minimal history.

### Corollary 7.1 — no local residual circuit

After quotienting by involutivity, contextual squares, and contextual pentagons, no nontrivial five-leaf atom circuit remains.

## 8. Van Kampen reduction of a global fixed-order loop

Let

\[
\Gamma
\]

be a closed fixed-order contextual scheduling history that produces no:

- route change;
- separator/category exit;
- strict cancellation;
- bounded terminal;
- smaller contextual target.

Forget root labels and the atom token. The projected history

\[
\overline\Gamma
\]

is a closed path in the ordinary Ptolemy flip groupoid.

By External Theorem 2.1, choose a finite van Kampen reduction of `overline Gamma` to the empty path using involution, square, and pentagon 2-cells.

Lift the reduction cell by cell.

1. An involution cell deletes by Theorem 4.1.
2. A square cell commutes by Theorem 5.1.
3. A pentagon cell lifts by Theorem 6.1.

Under the standing assumption that no exit occurs, each lifted cell either:

- replaces one root-valued path by another;
- moves the unique atom without branching;
- or deletes an identity `C6` lap.

If the atom were to return to the same enriched state after a nonempty lifted reduction, its local closed sector would be `C5` or `C6` by the complete five-leaf circuit theorem. The first is forbidden and the second is erasable. Therefore the lifted reduction reaches the empty contextual path.

### Theorem 8.1 — no fixed-order contextual monodromy

Every closed same-order contextual history with fixed outer route is trivial modulo the established local relations.

Equivalently, no nontrivial one-atom scheduling loop exists at fixed cap-target order.

## 9. Finite-state termination

Fix a finite cap-context graph order `N` and a fixed finite set of exterior incidences.

There are only finitely many:

- cubic multigraph combinatorial types on at most `N` labelled source vertices;
- root assignments from the ten roots to their edges;
- locations and labels of one optional zero/co-root atom;
- local pairing and resolution-sheet states.

Hence a deterministic normalized same-order scheduling procedure either terminates or repeats one contextual state.

A repetition would give a nontrivial closed same-order contextual history, contradicting Theorem 8.1 after deleting an immediate zero-length repetition.

### Theorem 9.1 — fixed-order termination

Every normalized same-order contextual transfer terminates after finitely many steps in one of:

1. complete root-valued inverse transfer;
2. route-changing matching flip and original-cap lift;
3. separator/category exit;
4. bounded direct-pairing terminal;
5. a first failure lying behind at least one equal-face cancellation, hence a strictly smaller cap-context target by the corrected order theorem.

There is no sixth same-order outcome.

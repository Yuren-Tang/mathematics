## 1. The six-root boundary universe

Let

\[
R_5=\{e_i+e_j:1\le i<j\le5\}
\subset E_5
\]

be the ten roots. Define the ordered conserved six-root boundary space

\[
\mathcal W_6
=
\left\{
(r_1,\ldots,r_6)\in R_5^6:
\sum_{i=1}^6 r_i=0
\right\}.
\]

Exact enumeration gives

\[
\boxed{|\mathcal W_6|=62560.}
\]

Let `\mathcal T_6` be the set of unrooted binary cubic trees with six labelled leaves. Then

\[
\boxed{|\mathcal T_6|=(2\cdot6-5)!!=105.}
\]

Every `T in \mathcal T_6` has four internal cubic vertices and three internal edges. Each internal edge determines a leaf split

\[
A\mid A^c,
\qquad
2\le |A|\le3,
\]

and for a boundary word `r=(r_1,\ldots,r_6)` its forced internal value is

\[
\lambda_{T,r}(A)
=
\sum_{i\in A}r_i.
\]

Because the total boundary sum is zero, the complementary shore gives the same value.

### Definition 1.1 — root completion

A labelled tree `T` is a root completion of `r` when every one of its three forced internal values lies in `R_5`.

Write

\[
\mathcal R(r)
=
\{T\in\mathcal T_6:T\text{ is a root completion of }r\}.
\]

### Theorem 2.1 — `59400/3160` dichotomy

Among the `62560` conserved ordered six-root boundary words:

\[
\boxed{
59400

\mathcal R(r)\ne\varnothing,
}
\]

and

\[
\boxed{
3160
\text{ satisfy }

}
\]

For every root-completable boundary,

\[
\boxed{
18\le |\mathcal R(r)|\le32.
}
\]

Thus root completability is never supported by one accidental topology. Every soluble boundary has a substantial finite family of root-valued tree realizations.

### Exact certificate

The enumeration runs over all pairs

\[
(r,T)\in\mathcal W_6\times\mathcal T_6.

For each of the three internal splits of `T`, it computes the xor of the root labels on one shore and tests whether the result has Hamming weight two. The classification is exact over the finite set

\[
62560\cdot105
\]

of boundary/topology pairs.

## 3. Classification of all non-root-completable boundaries

The `3160` exceptional ordered words have exactly three multiplicity types.

### Type S6 — one root repeated six times

\[
\boxed{a,a,a,a,a,a.}
\]

There are

\[
\boxed{10}
\]

ordered words of this form.

Every internal split sum lies in

\[
\{0,a\}.
\]

The minimum topology defect energy is two. Hence this class is entirely in the equality/root line generated by `a`.

### Type S42 — four copies of one root and two copies of a disjoint root

\[
\boxed{a,a,a,a,b,b,
\qquad a\cap b=\varnothing,}
\]

up to ordering.

There are

\[
\boxed{450}
\]

ordered words of this form.

Indeed, choose the fourfold root `a`, one of its three disjoint roots `b`, and the two positions occupied by `b`:

\[
10\cdot3\cdot\binom62=450.
\]

Every forced internal value belongs to the four-point plane

\[
\boxed{
\{0,a,b,a+b\},
}
\]

where `a+b` is the co-root on the four support indices of `a\cup b`. The minimum topology defect energy is two.

Thus this class is exactly one equality/DDD plane; it has no transverse or higher-rank coefficient value.

### Type S222 — three doubled roots in the odd-crossing configuration

The remaining exceptional words have the form

\[
\boxed{a,a,b,b,c,c}
\]

up to ordering, where the three distinct roots satisfy the unique exceptional intersection pattern represented by

\[
\boxed{12,12,13,13,45,45.}
\]

Equivalently, among the three unordered pairs of roots exactly one pair intersects, and the total singular pairing data lie in the corresponding zero/DDD fibre.

There are

\[
\boxed{2700}
\]

ordered words of this type.

Their minimum topology defect energy is one. Every minimum topology has internal weight pattern

\[
\boxed{(0,2,2)}
\]

or

\[
\boxed{(2,2,4).}
\]

Hence the unique defect is respectively:

- one equality/zero edge;
- one DDD/co-root edge with two crossed root resolutions.

### Theorem 3.1 — no fourth exceptional family

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal R(r)=\varnothing
\Longleftrightarrow
r\text{ belongs to Type S6, S42, or S222}.}
\]

The counts exhaust the exceptional set:

\[
10+450+2700=3160.
\]

Consequently every non-root-completable six-root boundary is already contained in the established equality/DDD singular alphabet.

## 4. Exterior component partitions

Let a four-vertex six-terminal tree region be removed from a connected bridgeless cubic multigraph. Its six terminal semiedges are labelled incidences; several incidences may meet the same exterior vertex.

The connected components of the exterior graph induce a set partition

\[
\Pi
\]

of the six terminal incidences.

Because the original graph is bridgeless, no exterior component can contain exactly one terminal incidence. Therefore every block of `\Pi` has size at least two.

The possible block-size patterns are

\[
6,
\qquad
4+2,
\qquad
3+3,
\qquad
2+2+2.
\]

The complete number of labelled set partitions is

\[
\boxed{
1+15+10+15=41.
}
\]

## 5. Bridge criterion for an arbitrary six-leaf tree replacement

Let `T` be any labelled six-leaf cubic tree and form the replacement graph by attaching its leaves to the six exterior terminal incidences.

Every terminal edge remains nonbridging: the exterior component containing its incidence contains at least one other terminal incidence and hence reconnects to the tree elsewhere.

Consider one internal edge `e` of `T`, with terminal split

\[
A_e\mid A_e^c.
\]

### Lemma 5.1 — internal bridge criterion

\[
\boxed{
e\text{ is a bridge of the replacement graph}
\iff
A_e\text{ is a union of blocks of }\Pi.
}
\]

### Proof

Deleting `e` separates the replacement tree into shores carrying terminal sets `A_e` and `A_e^c`. The two shores are reconnected through the exterior graph exactly when some exterior component contains terminal incidences on both sides. This fails precisely when every exterior component lies wholly on one shore, equivalently when `A_e` is a union of blocks of `\Pi`. ∎

### Definition 5.2 — `\Pi`-safe tree

A labelled tree `T` is `\Pi`-safe if none of its three internal splits is a union of blocks of `\Pi`.

By Lemma 5.1, a root-valued `\Pi`-safe tree gives a connected bridgeless replacement graph.

## 6. Universal bridgeless root replacement

### Theorem 6.1 — no attachment obstruction in the soluble sector

Let

\[
r\in\mathcal W_6
\]

be root-completable, and let `\Pi` be any one of the `41` possible exterior component partitions with no singleton block. Then there exists

\[
T\in\mathcal R(r)
\]

which is `\Pi`-safe.

Equivalently:

\[
\boxed{
\forall r\text{ with }\mathcal R(r)\ne\varnothing,
\quad
\forall\Pi,
\quad
\exists T:
T\text{ is root-valued and the replacement is bridgeless}.}
\]

More strongly, the exact exhaustive minimum is

\[
\boxed{
\min_{r,\Pi}
\#\{T\in\mathcal R(r):T\text{ is }\Pi\text{-safe}\}
\ge7.
}
\]

Thus no root-completable boundary is saved by a unique fragile topology. Even under the worst exterior attachment partition, at least seven root-valued bridgeless replacements remain.

### Exact finite certificate

For each of the

\[
59400
\]

root-completable boundary words and each of the

\[
41
\]

allowed exterior partitions, enumerate its root-valued trees and test their three internal splits against the block-union criterion of Lemma 5.1.

No exception occurs.

The statement treats terminal incidences rather than exterior vertices and therefore includes arbitrary coincidences of terminal endpoints and all parallel-edge degeneracies allowed in the cubic multigraph category.

## 7. Secondary-defect consequence

Choose a connected bridgeless cubic counterexample lexicographically minimal for

\[
\boxed{(|V(G)|,\delta(G)),}
\]

where `\delta(G)` is the minimum number of zero/co-root edges among all `E_5` flows on `G`.

Suppose an `E_5` flow on `G` contains a four-vertex tree region whose six exterior incidences are root-valued and whose internal edges include at least one defect.

Let `r` be its ordered six-root boundary word.

### Corollary 7.1 — ordinary boundary exclusion

If

\[
\mathcal R(r)\ne\varnothing,
\]

then Theorem 6.1 supplies a same-order connected bridgeless root-valued tree replacement. The inherited exterior flow extends over the replacement with strictly fewer defects and the same vertex count.

The replacement graph therefore cannot be a counterexample by secondary minimality. A root cover of the replacement graph may be pulled back through the original four-vertex tree as an `E_5` flow, giving the usual defect-localisation consequence.

Hence an irreducible four-vertex root-boundary defect region must have

\[
\boxed{
\mathcal R(r)=\varnothing.
}
\]

### Corollary 7.2 — singular-boundary localisation

Every irreducible four-vertex defect region with six root-valued exterior incidences has boundary of Type S6, S42, or S222.

Thus its coefficient data lie entirely in:

- one equality/root line;
- one equality/DDD four-point plane;
- or one single zero/DDD singular edge with two crossed root resolutions.

There is no irreducible generic six-root boundary.

## 8. Relation to the pivot-path theorem

For the standard pivot-change boundary

\[
(12,35,45\mid25,34,15),
\]

the boundary is in the root-completable sector. Indeed it has twenty root-valued labelled trees, including the four explicit three-cherry stars.

Theorem 6.1 strengthens the earlier star-specific source theorem:

- it allows all `105` labelled six-leaf topologies;
- it applies to all `59400` root-completable boundary words;
- it handles all `41` exterior component partitions;
- it guarantees at least seven bridgeless root replacements in every case.

For the pivot boundary, this supplies exactly the category input used by secondary defect minimality to force

\[
\delta(G)=3
\]

and localise the complete defect support to the three-co-root path. The endpoint-equalisation theorem then eliminates that normal form.

## 9. Revised proof-DAG role

This theorem removes the need to realise an arbitrarily prescribed lowering NNI move in the source graph whenever the six exterior boundary incidences are roots.

Instead:

\[
\boxed{
\text{root-completable boundary}
\Longrightarrow
\text{some bridgeless root tree replacement}.}
\]

The only remaining six-leaf source cases are the three singular repeated-root families, already expressed in the equality/DDD alphabet.

Therefore the remaining global bridge is narrower:

> In a defect-minimal induced forest, prove that either one can expose a four-vertex subtree with six root-valued exterior incidences, or the component is already an equality/DDD singular carrier, a rank-one/rank-two side network, or a small-separator branch.

This is an enclosure/extraction theorem, not a six-port topology-classification problem.
